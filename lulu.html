01

The
Brand

Most investors who ignore clothing retail would be surprised to learn that Lululemon's gross margin sits above 58 percent — higher than Apple's in some quarters, and nearly 15 points above Nike. This is not a conventional retailer. It is a vertically integrated, direct-to-consumer brand with full control over pricing, distribution, and the customer relationship. It does not wholesale. It does not discount. Its return on equity has not dipped below 20 percent in over a decade. Its 28 million members return at a rate of 89 to 92 percent. These facts, taken together with the market's near-consensus pessimism about LULU's prospects, pointed to one of two conclusions. In 2026, the market was pricing this business at 11 times earnings against a 10-year average multiple above 42. Either LULU was a broken company or a deeply misread one.

02

The
Misread

The surface narrative in 2025 and 2026 was straightforward: North American growth had slowed, product assortment had grown stale, and consumer spending was under pressure. The stock fell accordingly from highs above $500 to under $150. Yet the market was overlooking Lululemon's balance sheet. Tangible book value per share had doubled over three years. Cash holdings of approximately $1.5 billion sat alongside negligible financial debt. Michael Burry, famed 'Big Short' investor, having reviewing the same financials, noted it was among the least pessimistic sell-side consensuses he had seen for a company in that financial condition. China revenue, meanwhile, continued growing at double digits. For patient value investors, the fundamentals were unequivocably telling a different story.

03

The
Conviction

Lululemon has survived the death of its founder's vision, a pandemic, a CEO departure, and now a prolonged consumer spending drought — and through all of it, 28 million people kept showing up. This brand continues to prove the market wrong about its stickiness. The position was initiated on April 26, 2026, at $143.80. When the stock declined further, a second tranche was added on June 18, 2026, at $111.84. We feel comfortable about our margin of safety entering at these low prices for a quality company. A business with negligible debt, $1.5 billion in cash, 89 percent customer retention, and a still-nascent international footprint does not deserve to trade at distressed multiples. The market continues to offer us the opportunity to own one of the most defensible consumer brands of the last two decades, and we will oblige.
