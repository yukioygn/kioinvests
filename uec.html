UEC — Full price history

01

The
Wasteland

Fukushima did not just damage a reactor. It repriced an entire industry. After March 2011, uranium collapsed from $70 toward $18 per pound — a decade-long bear market driven by fear, not fundamentals. Miners abandoned projects. Capital fled. Then COVID arrived and finished the job, cratering energy demand and forcing the last holdout producers to cut output. The sector looked uninvestable by every conventional measure. But the world's 440 operating reactors never stopped needing fuel. Uranium cannot be substituted. Reactors cannot run on sentiment. The market had confused a cycle with a conclusion.

02

The
Position

The position was initiated on March 19, 2021, at $2.76. This was not a prediction, but a recognition of asymmetry. Uranium at $29 per pound sat below the all-in cost of production for most miners. A commodity cannot trade below its cost of production indefinitely. UEC was chosen for its in-situ recovery model — the lowest-cost extraction method in the sector — with permitted, shovel-ready assets in Texas and Wyoming. At $2.76, the stock assumed no recovery whatsoever. The downside was structurally capped while the upside required only that the world continue to need nuclear power.

03

The
Exit

The position was exited in two stages. Half was sold on April 5, 2021 at $3.50 — seventeen days after entry, recovering initial capital at a 26.8% return. The remaining half was held into 2023 as uranium staged a broader recovery, exited on September 11, 2023 at $5.00, an 81.2% return on that tranche. The blended return across both exits was approximately 54 percent. Exiting in 2023 was deliberate. The next leg of the uranium trade required variables — the 2024 election, geopolitical energy policy, Sprott-driven spot dynamics — that lay outside the original thesis.

04

The Reflection

UEC has traded above $10 in 2026. The uranium story did not end in 2023, but rather accelerated. Russia's invasion of Ukraine permanently disrupted global uranium supply chains. The artificial intelligence boom created an enormous and entirely unforeseen source of electricity demand, driving technology companies toward nuclear power agreements with an urgency no one had modelled. None of this was visible in 2021 or even 2023. The original thesis was correct. But what followed was not the modest cycle recovery the thesis anticipated. There is now a structural repricing of nuclear energy's role in the global economy. The volatility of the position across that two-and-a-half year hold was considerable — and at the time of exit, the next leg of the trade required variables that lay outside the original thesis entirely. Speculation is not prophecy.
